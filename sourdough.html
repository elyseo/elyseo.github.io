Where It Started

My Sourdough Story

✍️

How I Started

It started during the COVID pandemic — I wanted to do something. I've always loved baking, and during the pandemic I wanted to bake more than just sweets. So I started doing research on sourdough: what it required to take care of and if I could even do it. After a bunch of research I decided it was time to start my sourdough journey. I got my ingredients together, flour and water. I added them together and then had to continue that for about 14 days. My research had indicated that after the initial 14 days I could start making sourdough. Well, even though I had done research I wasn't sure how to know if my starter was mature. So I just kept adding flour and water every day, a 1:1:1 ratio (we'll talk more about the ratios later). Eventually I got discouraged and ended up throwing it away. I went back to baking sweets and I loved it.

After a couple years I decided it was time for me to buckle down and really try to make sourdough. My friends had started making sourdough and showed me pictures, and I really wanted some homemade bread! So I asked my friend for some of her starter, thinking it would be easier. Plus I had read that the more mature the starter the better the bread. It was going really well — I had made some sourdough discard recipes like pancakes and a few other things, and they were amazing! I decided one morning that that would be the day I officially started making sourdough. It was winter time, so to make sure my starter had the right environment I put it in the oven with the light on. Now it was time to wait. Well, unfortunately we forgot my starter was in the oven and started preheating it for dinner. It was too late to go back — my starter was baked. That happened twice! I was discouraged so I stopped my sourdough journey.

The next winter my friend gave me another starter. I wasn't going to be scared to bake sourdough anymore. Within a week my starter had peaked and I started making dough. After 4 years of attempting to make sourdough I finally had made one! It wasn't perfect but it was delicious. In two weeks I had made 13 loaves of sourdough! I was having so much fun experimenting with different recipes and add-ins. Then I started making different things with my starter — bagels, focaccia, and so many other things. I loved it all!

Now that you know my journey, what's your story? What makes you want to bake? Are you just starting your sourdough journey? Are you in that stage of "I've tried so many times and just don't know if I should continue"? Are you looking for new and exciting recipes? If you answer yes to any of those questions then you are in the right place. Welcome to Baking Frenzy!

The Foundation

How I Care for My Starter

🌱

My Starter Routine

I don't bake sourdough very much so I keep my starter in the fridge. When I'm ready to bake I take it out 3 days before I want to actually have bread. I keep 10 grams of starter in the fridge. I feed my starter a 1:4:4 ratio — that means my 10 grams of starter gets 40 grams of flour and 40 grams of water. I do that around 7am and then again around 7pm. I do that two days in a row. Then on the third day I start my dough.

Signs My Starter Is Ready to Bake

  • Doubled or more in size since last feeding
  • Domed top — flat or sunken means it's past peak
  • Lots of bubbles visible through the jar sides
  • Smells pleasantly tangy and yeasty, not acetone-sharp
  • Passes the float test — a small spoonful floats in water
  • Pulls away from the jar edges and looks stringy inside
The Weekly Bake

My Bake Schedule

I bake on weekends. Here's the rough timeline I follow — adjust it for your kitchen temperature.

My typical weekend bake timeline. Times are approximate — let the dough, not the clock, guide you.
Day & Time Stage What I Do What to Look For Duration
Fri · 8 PM Feed Starter Feed at 1:4:4 ratio (starter:flour:water). Place on a heating pad at 75–78°F. Bubbles forming within 2 hrs ~12 hrs
Sat · 7 AM Autolyse Mix flour + water + starter together Shaggy dough, no dry patches 30–60 min
Sat · 9 AM Add Salt Add salt and incorporate fully Dough feels cohesive 15 min
Sat · 9–1 PM Bulk Fermentation 4 sets of stretch & fold, then rest undisturbed Dough 50–75% larger, jiggly, bubbly 4–5 hrs
Sat · 1 PM Preshape & Shape Bench rest, then final shaping into banneton Tight surface tension 30 min
Sat · 1:30 PM Cold Retard Cover & refrigerate overnight Dough feels firm and cold 8–16 hrs
Sun · 8 AM Preheat & Score Preheat Dutch oven to 350°F, score loaf Confident, clean score lines 45 min preheat
Sun · 9 AM Bake 25 min lid-on, then 15–25 min lid-off Deep brown crust, hollow thump 40–50 min
Total active time: ~2 hours. Total elapsed time: ~36 hours. Best results come from patience, not rushing.
📅

Personal Schedule Notes

Depending on your kitchen temperature your timing might be a little different. You might have to wait longer for your starter to peak and your dough to reach the ideal texture. My oven runs hot, so I keep the temperature lower — usually 350°F. I also like to put a few ice cubes in my Dutch oven for the first 25 minutes; it creates more steam, allowing for a better spring.

The Main Event

My Sourdough Loaf Recipe

My Country Sourdough

A simple, open-crumb loaf with a crackly crust.

Yield 1 loaf
Hydration 75%
Levain % 20%
Active Time ~2 hrs
Total Time ~36 hrs
Difficulty Intermediate

Ingredients

  • Levain (build night before)
    • 40 grams starter
    • 80 grams flour
    • 80 grams water
  • Final Dough
    • 475 grams all purpose flour
    • 325 grams water
    • 10 grams salt
    • 100 grams active starter (levain)

Method

📝

My Method

Feed the starter 4–8 hours before starting your dough. Then make your dough — add starter, flour, water, and salt together in a bowl. Mix until no dry spots are seen. Cover and wait 30 minutes.

Start your first set of stretch and folds: pick up one side of the dough and pull up about 6 inches, then place it into the center. Turn the bowl and pull up on the other side. Do that two more times around the bowl. Cover and wait 30 minutes. Do another set. Cover and rest 30 minutes, then finish your last set of stretch and folds.

Let it sit in a warm place for 4–8 hours until it's doubled in size. After bulk fermentation, place the dough on the counter and slightly stretch it, fold into itself, and roll up. Pinch any seams. Transfer to a floured banneton or a bowl lined with a floured tea towel. Refrigerate overnight, 8–14 hours. This makes it easier to score and gives it more of that sourdough tang.

Put your Dutch oven in the oven and preheat to 450°F. Place the sourdough onto a piece of parchment and score it. Lower into the Dutch oven, add a few ice cubes, and put the lid on. Bake for 25 minutes. Remove the lid and bake for another 15–25 minutes until deep golden brown.

💡 Tips

Make sure your starter passes the float test before baking — if it doesn't, your sourdough won't be light and full of bubbles. If your dough is too sticky, wet your hands when performing stretch and folds. Don't worry if your dough is taking longer to double in size; depending on your environment it might take more or less time.

Reflections

My Thoughts on Sourdough

💭

Reflections

It can be challenging — there are so many recipes, ratios, and opinions on sourdough. But once you find what works for you, the timing, temperature, and recipe that clicks, you won't ever be able to go back to store-bought. Sourdough can be intimidating, but you can do it!
